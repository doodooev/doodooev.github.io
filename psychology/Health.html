Patient-Practitioner Relationship

McKinstry and Wang
  • Investigate if doctors' dressing style is important.
  • Wanted to see if there were demographic differences in the beliefs.
  • 475 Participants from Scotland completed interviews after visiting the doctor.
  • “Which doctor would you feel happiest about seeing for the first time?” Score from 0-5.
  • Asked about confidence in the ability of the doctors.
  • Asked if they would be unhappy consulting them.
  • Asked which one looks most like your doctor.
  • Asked closed questions about their dressing sense and attitudes to different clothes.
  • 5 photos of men, 3 photos of women. Fewer photos of women since they had less distinct styles.
  • Both models had the same pose for each photo.
  • Results: Doctor in a smart suit was most popular. Tweed jacket and Tie – 2nd most popular. Jeans – Least popular.
  • Female doctor in traditional clothing – most popular. Scores for women were higher than men.
  • Older patients gave higher scores to both genders in the white coat.
  • 64% of patients thought the way their doctor dressed was important.
McKinlay
  • Investigate the perceived and actual comprehension of medical terms in lower working-class families.
  • Researchers spent a month around doctors in gynaecology wards and collected 57 words.
  • Doctors crossed off words that they don’t use often, resulting in 13 final words.
  • In interviews, they were sounded out and used in a sentence.
  • Participants asked to explain their understanding, which was recorded and reviewed by 2 doctors for accuracy.
  • 18 other doctors were given the words and asked if they expect the lower class to understand.
  • Compared the understanding of Under-Utilisers and Utilisers.
  • Utilisers had a better understanding of words (11/13).
  • Doctors underestimated mothers' understanding.
  • The term “Breach” was understood by most mothers, but doctors assumed only 22% of patients would understand.
  • There is a tendency for doctors to use jargon without the expectation of patients understanding.
Patients might disclose information inaccurately because:
  • They are angry.
  • Ignore the doctor's requests.
  • Insist on false medication.
Robinson and West
  • Patients give more information to computerized interviews rather than questionnaires.
  • Both methods gave more info than speaking face-to-face with a doctor.
False Positive / Type 1
  • Patient is healthy, diagnosed as sick.
False Negative / Type 2
  • Patient is sick, diagnosed as healthy.
Cooke and Colver
  • Investigated how patients with skin cancer would like to receive a diagnosis.
  • Letter: Can be read anytime. No need to go to the hospital. Takes longer to be typed and sent. Not personal. Can't ask questions.
  • Telephone: Quick. No need to go to the hospital. Can be done from home. Can be difficult to have a conversation. May not be available during the time of the call.
  • Face to Face: More personal. Can ask questions. Can have a relative alongside. Need to go to the hospital.
  • 52% of patients preferred a letter over face-to-face.
Schofield
  • Sent a questionnaire to patients 4 months after skin cancer diagnosis.
  • No differences in satisfaction or depression between diagnosis by phone or face-to-face.
  • Higher satisfaction when patients were prepared for diagnosis.
  • Higher satisfaction when the word "cancer" was used, and when information was presented clearly.
Safer et al.
  • Appraisal Delay: Time taken to judge a symptom as an illness.
  • Experience of the symptom.
  • Observing for change.
  • Emotional reaction to the symptom.
  • Imagined consequence.
  • Illness Delay: Time taken from deciding illness and deciding to seek help.
  • Emotional factors.
  • Beliefs about illness and medical services.
  • Utilisation Delay: Time taken from deciding to seek help and actually seeking help.
  • Demographic and situational factors.
  • 4 total clinics, 93 patients, and a 45-minute questionnaire and interview.
  • Wide range of age and ethnicity, slightly more females than males.
  • Interview designed to measure all types of delay and total time for each stage.
  • Questionnaire had a mix of open and closed questions, used Likert-type scales.
  • Results: No correlation between the length of each delay stage. Mean total delay was 57 days. Most significant variable was “change in life unrelated to medical condition” such as marriage or divorce. Those with this variable reported significantly later. If the symptom was painful, the patient would report much sooner. If the patient read about their symptom, they reported much later. Under 45-year-olds would report sooner, over 45 delayed.

Adherence

Primary Non-Adherence
  • Doctor writes a prescription, medication is never collected.
Non-Persistence
  • Patient takes medication but stops midway. Can often be unintentional.
Non-Conforming
  • Medication is not taken as prescribed.
Problems of Non-Adherence
  • Waste of medication.
  • Progression of illness.
  • Lower quality of life.
Laba et al.
  • Investigate what factors contribute to rational non-adherence.
  • 161 Australian participants, wide range of education levels and income.
  • Online survey with 3 parts.
  • Section 1: Questions on current medication use and attitudes towards medication.
  • Section 2: Discrete Choice Experiment (DCE). 10 hypothetical situations presented. Participants had to choose one of the two. Asked to imagine if they’re taking 2 medications. Asked which medication they would be most happy taking. Factors for each medication were provided such as: How it is taken, Alcohol restrictions, Monthly cost.
  • Section 3: Background information on the patient.
  • Results: Symptom severity and alcohol restrictions did NOT have a significant effect on adherence. Background characteristics had no significant difference in the results. Monthly cost of medication was the most significant factor for those without private medical insurance. Patients were willing to trade factors such as, they would prefer taking the medication once a day compared to four times a day, although they would take it four times if it reduced the risk of unwanted side effects.
Measuring Non-Adherence
  • MAM/Medical Adherence Measure: Semi-structured clinical interview to collect data on patients' adherence.
Reikert and Drotar
  • Those who refuse to participate in a study about treatment adherence would show less adherence.
  • Adolescents, 11-18 years old, had type 1 diabetes, at least one family member.
  • 52 participants completed the whole study.
  • 28 agreed to take part but failed to return the questionnaire.
  • 14 completed the interview but refused further questions.
  • The child had to complete a semi-structured interview which led to an overall adherence score.
  • Parents filled out a demographic information sheet.
  • A questionnaire which would take approx. 30 min to complete was given to the parent, asked about their relationship with their child.
  • A questionnaire which would take approx. 1 hour to complete was given to the child, asked about their relationship with the parent.
  • Demographics between the 3 groups did not differ much other than fathers' education, which was higher for the participant group.
  • Those who didn’t return the questionnaire did fewer blood sugar checks per day. They also scored lower on the adherence interview.
Chung and Naya
  • Electronically measuring compliance in asthma patients using MEMS.
  • 12-week trial, 57 patients given 3 weeks of medicine.
  • After 3 weeks, patients return the bottle to be filled with the next 3 weeks of tablets.
  • Instructed to take 2 tablets a day, 12 hours apart.
  • MEMS cap tracked time and date of opening.
  • Insufficient Interval Adherence: Opening at intervals of less than 8 hours.
  • Undercompliance: Days with only one event.
  • No Compliance: Days with no events.
  • Over Compliance: Days with more than two events.
  • 66% of patients had good compliance.
  • Mean time between tablets was 12 hours 34 minutes.
  • Two patients suspected tracking and removed tablets to conceal non-compliance.
While contracts can be used to improve adherence, there is little evidence to show any long-term improvement.
  • Prompts such as text messages, calls can be used to improve adherence.
  • Customizing treatment can improve adherence, such as turning a twice-daily dosage into a once-daily dosage.
Chaney et al.
  • Children do not adhere partly due to the fault of parents (can cause them stress) and partly due to the child.
  • Funhaler had a whistle and toy element.
  • 32 children, 1.5-6 years old, with asthma.
  • 2-week study.
  • Parents filled out a close-ended questionnaire about attitudes and adherence of their children.
  • Funhaler was provided, and the same questionnaire was given after 2 weeks.
  • Parents were phone-called to ask if the child had taken treatment on the previous day.
  • 59% of children had been medicated with a normal inhaler.
  • 81% of children had been medicated with the Funhaler.
  • When using standard treatment, children only completed the breath cycles correctly 50% of the time compared to 80% with the Funhaler.
  • When using a standard spacer, 10% of parents reported it being always successful, this increased to 73% with the Funhaler.
  • Fear was a common response to a normal spacer; there was no report of fear with the Funhaler.
Yokley and Glenwick
  • Aim: Evaluate the effectiveness of 4 different conditions for motivating parents to get their preschool children immunized.
  • Mailed general prompt.
  • Mailed specific prompt.
  • Mailed specific prompt and expanded clinic hours.
  • Mailed specific prompt and monetary incentive (cash lottery).
  • Design: Carried out on the entire population of non-immunized preschool clients, less than 5 years of age, at a public health clinic in America.
  • 1133 children required at least ONE immunization. Even number of boys and girls.
  • General prompt group was mailed a prompt urging parents to get their children immunized without any personal information.
  • Specific prompt group was mailed a prompt that named the child and the immunization required, giving clinic location and hours.
  • Increased access group received the same prompt as the specific prompt group as well as information about extra out-of-hours sessions at the clinic, with childcare facilities, and the perk of being able to leave their children at the clinic for the full session.
  • Monetary incentive group received the same prompt as the specific prompt as well as information on a cash lottery, offering a ticket when the child was taken for immunization.
  • 2 control groups: Contact control group received a telephone call providing information on immunization without a prompt. No contact group was not contacted at all during the study.
  • Dependent Variables: Number of children receiving one or more immunizations. Number of target children attending the clinic for any reason. Total number of immunizations received by target children.
  • Results: Monetary incentive group had the biggest impact, followed by increased access group, specific prompt group, general prompt group, and then the control groups. Findings remained significant after both the two-month and three-month follow-up. Specific prompt was the most cost-effective method. Monetary incentive being least cost-effective long-term. Increased access was considered least cost-effective in the beginning, although it became more cost-effective than the monetary incentive group.
  • Advantages: Data was representative of the entire target population. Longitudinal study with 2 and 3-month follow-up measures.

Pain

MacLachlan et al.
  • Case study on Phantom Limb Pain (PLP).
  • 32-year-old man named Alan.
  • After amputating his leg, he began to experience phantom limb pain.
  • Pain was mild initially, felt as if two of his toes were crossed.
  • Experienced “pins and needles” in his toes.
  • By late afternoon, the pain was severe.
  • Felt as though his leg was shorter than the other, as if it was stretching backward with toes pointing downward.
  • Tried pain medication and TENS.
  • Followed the procedure of mirror treatment.
  • Ten repetitions of 10 exercises with a physiotherapist for a few days.
  • Exercises were carried out up to four times a day, for three weeks.
  • In the second week, the feeling of crossed toes decreased.
  • By the end of the third week, there was no sensation of crossed toes, and phantom pain was minimal.
Specificity Theory
  • Specialized pain receptors in the body respond to sensations, sending signals to the brain. The brain processes the signal and registers it as pain, sending motor responses to stop the pain.
Gate Control Theory
  • Small nerve fibers are responsible for sensations such as pain.
  • Large nerve fibers are responsible for sensations such as touch, pressure, and other skin sensations.
  • The spinal cord contains a gate that either prevents pain signals from entering or allows them to continue.
  • If you hurt your arm, you might trigger the small nerve fibers; you will then proceed to rub your arm involuntarily. This is because it activates the larger nerve fibers, reducing the sensation of pain received in the brain.
Subjective Measures of Measuring Pain
  • Clinical Interview: Ask patient open-ended questions and get an understanding of their experience.
  • McGill Pain Questionnaire: Contains 78 words; the patient chooses the word that best describes their pain. The words are assigned a value based on their intensity, and the patient is given a total score from 0-78.
  • Visual Analogue Scale: Instrument that lets the patient mark on a line that best represents their pain.
Brudvik et al.
  • Research has shown that parents and doctors often undermine the pain that children feel.
  • Aim: Investigate the level of agreement of pain intensity when measured by children, parents, and physicians.
  • Estimate the influence of children's age, medical condition, and severity of pain given by children, parents, and physicians.
  • See how pain assessments affected physicians' administration of medication.
  • Design: 243 children, 3-15 years, almost equal boys and girls. They attended a Norwegian emergency department over 17 days. The patients, their parents, and the doctors themselves completed a questionnaire. 51 different doctors; half of them had children of their own. Half of them had over 5 years of experience.
  • Children 3-8 years used the Faces Pain Rating Scale.
  • Children 9-15 years used the Visual Analogue Scale and Coloured Analogue Scale.
  • Parents and physicians completed the Numeric Rating Scale to estimate the level of pain from 0-10.
  • Parents and children were instructed not to tell the physicians their ratings.
  • Results: Doctors assessed the mean pain of the child to be 3.2. Parents assessed the mean pain of the child to be 4.8. The child's own mean pain was 5.5. Only 14.3% of children with severe pain received painkillers.
  • Evaluation: Large sample, wide range of ages and conditions. Doctors with a range of experience and backgrounds. High ecological validity.
  • Self-report can give a false representation of actual feelings. Doctors might change their assessments just because they know they’re in a study.
  • Providing children with more painkillers could reduce their anxiety of hospitals or doctors.
UAB Pain Behaviour Scale
  • Scale with 10 target behaviors. Observer completes the scale, recording detail about each of the target behaviors noticed.
Managing Pain
  • Biological Treatments: Analgesics (Painkillers), Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (Analgesic that reduces pain by reducing the production of hormones that cause pain), Opioids (Analgesic which works by attaching opioid receptors to the brain. These cells then release signals that reduce the perception of pain and increase pleasure).
  • Psychological Treatments: Attention Diversion (Puzzles, massage, crafting, music), Non-Pain Imagery (Thinking about a calm and relaxing situation rather than focusing on the pain), Cognitive Redefinition (Replacing threatening negative thoughts about pain with rational positive thoughts).
  • Alternative Treatments: Acupuncture (Asian method, involves the insertion of fine needles at specific points. They release endorphins and increase levels of serotonin), TENS (Small machine, uses electrodes to deliver a mild current to the painful area. Impulses work by reducing pain signals that go to the brain and spinal cord. They also stimulate the production of endorphins).

Stress

GAS Model
  • 3-stage process that describes the biological changes in the body during stress.
  • Alarm: Cortisol (stress hormone) is released, as well as adrenaline. Increased heart rate, etc.
  • Resistance: Body returns to normal, starts to repair itself while staying on high alert. Cortisol continues to be secreted.
  • Exhaustion: If resistance or stress lingers for too long, it can lead to the exhaustion stage. The body is drained and can no longer fight stress effectively.
Homes and Rahe
  • Life events are a major cause of stress.
  • They created SRRS.
  • 2500 male American sailors did the SRRS. Asked how many major life events they experienced in the last 6 months. During the next 6 months, their health status was recorded.
  • As life change score increased, so did the frequency of illness.
Chandola et al.
  • Investigated the link between workplace stress and CHD.
  • Longitudinal study with over 10,000 male and female civil servants in London, from 1985-2004.
  • 7 phases, each phase included a questionnaire and a clinical examination.
  • Job Strain: High job demands and low job control.
  • Iso Strain: Job strain high plus social isolation at work.
  • Job strain questionnaire was used.
  • Total work stress score was assigned based on reported incidents of work or iso strain in phase 1 and 2.
  • 37-49-year-olds at phase 2 had a high association between work stress and CHD.
  • Little association of the same for those over 50 years of age.
  • For younger ages, higher work stress had a link with poor behaviors such as eating fewer fruits and vegetables.
Friedman and Roseman
  • Type A Personality: Competitive, hostile, fast-paced, adventurous.
  • Type B Personality: Relaxed, easy-going, tolerant.
  • Longitudinal study to test if Type A is at a higher chance of heart disease.
  • 3000 men, 39-59 years, given a questionnaire. Classified as Type A or B based on responses.
  • Men were followed for 8.5 years.
  • Findings showed that twice as many Type A men developed CHD.
  • 257/3000 men developed CHD, 70% of which were Type A.
Ways to Measure Stress
  • fMRI: Functional magnetic resonance imaging. Measures activity in the brain by tracking changes in the flow of blood. When a part of the brain is activated, it requires more blood, hence we can see how the brain reacts to different stimuli.
  • Oximeter: Clips on the finger that can be used to measure oxygen saturation.
Wang et al.
  • fMRI to measure changes in blood flow as a response to stress.
  • 30 Americans, 24 years avg, 11 females in the experimental group.
  • 7 Americans, 23 years average, 4 females in the control group.
  • Participants were given 4 MRI scans.
  • First MRI scan = Baseline scan.
  • Second MRI scan = Low-stress task = Counting aloud backward from 1000.
  • Third MRI scan = High-stress task = Subtracting 13 from a 4-digit number, answering aloud and being asked to speed up, starting again if an error is made.
  • Last MRI scan = Baseline scan.
  • Self-reports on stress from a scale of 1-9 were taken along with saliva samples to test for cortisol after each scan.
  • Heart rate was recorded throughout the study using an oximeter.
  • Control group did not complete any tasks.
  • All participants took part between 3 and 5 pm.
  • Self-reported stress, anxiety, heart rate, and cortisol levels all increased with the high-stress task, reduced during the 2nd baseline period.
  • The higher the stress, the higher the activity in the right prefrontal cortex.
Evans and Werner
  • Salivary cortisol tests as a measure of stress for people in crowded trains.
  • 139 adults who traveled from New Jersey into Manhattan.
  • Saliva sample was collected from each person at the end of their morning trip to work, and another at the same time on the weekend at home.
  • Motivation was measured at the end of the commute by a reading task (counting the number of errors detected).
  • Crowding in the whole carriage did not have a significant effect on stress.
  • Crowding in the immediate area had a significant effect on all 3 measures: cortisol level, proofreading, and mood.
Managing Stress
  • Budzyinski et al.: Using an analogue monitor to measure muscle tension on the forehead. 15 participants, asked to lie quietly with their eyes closed, asked to concentrate on relaxing their forehead. 20 one-minute trials, trials being over 3 days apart. Randomly assigned to one of three groups: Feedback Group (Monitor would make sounds based on how relaxed the forehead muscle is), Irrelevant Feedback Group (Monitor would give a constant low sound; told that the tone would help them relax), No Feedback Group (No tone was given; they were told to relax in silence). Those who received correct feedback had less muscle tension. Feedback group showed a tension decrease of a mean of 50%. No feedback group showed a tension decrease of a mean of 24%. Irrelevant feedback group showed a mean increase of 28%.
  • Bridge et al.: Evaluate the effect of relaxation and imagery on stress experienced by cancer patients. 139 women, undergoing 6 weeks of radiotherapy in a hospital in the UK, under the age of 70. Asked to fill out a questionnaire assessing their mood at the start of the program. Randomly assigned to one of three conditions: Control Group (No treatment), Relaxation Group (Taught a technique involving directing awareness to specific muscles, instructions for deep breathing), Relaxation + Imagery Group (Same as relaxation group and were taught to imagine a peaceful scene). The women were given a tape to be listened to for 15 minutes a day; it included all the instructions. All women were seen by a researcher for 30 minutes once a week for all six weeks. During this, those in the control group spoke about themselves and their interests; the others practiced their exercises. Used 2 questionnaires: Profile of Mood States Questionnaire (Uses 65 items to give scores over aspects such as tension, fatigue, anger, confusion), Leeds General Scale (6 items for anxiety and depression, each rated on a 4-point scale). Results: After 6 weeks, the most improvement in mood was with the relaxation and imagery group. Mood state for women in the control group worsened. Women with high anger levels in initial assessments did not respond well to relaxation or imagery compared to those with lower anger. Evaluation: All patients were women. All attending the same hospital for the same cancer. Illness was not a confounding variable, although the sample isn’t representative. Randomly allocated participants, high validity. Questionnaires didn’t gather qualitative data. High application to everyday life; can affect many patients. Can be used to advise other women suffering from cancer.
  • SIT – Stress Inoculation Training: Behavioral therapy designed to let people develop coping skills for stress. Conceptualization (Client and therapist try to find the source of the stress. The therapist tries to show the client how to reframe the stressor as a problem that can be solved), Skills Acquisition and Rehearsal (Client learns new coping skills, designed to suit their set of circumstances), Application and Follow Through (Client applies what they’ve learned in the real world).

Health Promotion

Janis and Feshbach
  • Fear arousal in dental hygiene education.
  • 3 different levels of fear to 200 high school students about dental hygiene.
  • One questionnaire a week before the lecture, another after the lecture, and another a week later.
  • Each group was shown a recorded lecture by the same speaker, same information, and same recommendations for oral hygiene.
  • Three groups with different amounts of fear arousal.
  • Form 1 – Strong Fear Appeal: Focused on painful consequences of tooth decay. Personalized threats such as “this could happen to you.” Slides included very graphic and realistic photos.
  • Form 2 – Moderate Fear Appeal: Dangers described in a milder manner. Fewer photos with milder examples.
  • Form 3 – Minimal Fear Appeal: Hardly any mention of negative consequences of poor oral hygiene. Information was shown about tooth growth and function. No realistic photos, only x-rays and charts.
  • A control group was given a lecture on a different topic.
  • All four groups were approximately equal in terms of age, sex, and IQ.
  • Emotional reactions immediately after the lectures varied a lot between the strong and minimal fear groups.
  • Level of Concern Score (out of 10): Strong fear group = 7.8/10. Moderate fear group = 6.6/10. Minimal fear group = 5.9/10.
  • Between the first and last questionnaire, those in the minimal fear group showed significantly more improvement in teeth cleaning.
  • Those in the strong fear group showed the least improvement.
  • At the talk, they were told about the importance of the right type of toothbrush.
  • The 3rd questionnaire had a false passage on how toothbrushes do not matter.
  • All students, regardless of their group, showed resistance to this contradictory statement, saying that the right type of toothbrush did matter.
Lewin et al.
  • Effect of giving information to help people improve their own health.
  • 190 participants, all had been admitted to the hospital after a heart attack.
  • 3 days after the incident, they had been assigned to either an experimental or control group.
  • Experimental Group: After discharge from the hospital, they were given a manual; it had 6 weekly sections of programs. It also had self-help treatments for anxiety, depression, etc. A facilitator explained the contents of the manual to the patient and their partner. After 1, 3, 6 weeks, contacted by the facilitator. Had a check-in on their progress and were encouraged to continue the exercises.
  • Control Group: They were also given time with the facilitator; given a range of leaflets that gave information about recovery from a heart attack. After 1, 3, 6 weeks, contacted by the facilitator. Given general encouragement and support.
  • Psychological health was measured via a questionnaire after 6 weeks, 6 months, and a year.
  • Anxiety levels had dropped significantly more for participants in the experimental group compared to the control group.
  • The number of those showing poor psychological health was significantly lower in the experimental group than the control group.
  • Those who were highly distressed during the heart attack had higher anxiety and depression in the control group after each follow-up.
Tapper et al.
  • Only 4% of 4-6-year-olds eat the recommended portions of fruits and vegetables per day.
  • 3 main techniques to change a child’s eating habits: Taste Exposure (Encourage children to keep trying new foods), Modelling (A child is likely to imitate someone they like, preferably someone older than them), Rewards (Rewards can encourage certain behaviors. Rewards are effective when they’re desirable and achievable).
  • Study was carried out at home with 5-6-year-old fussy eaters.
  • Peer modelling involved a video of older children, the “Food Dudes,” who gain superpowers from eating fruits and vegetables and battle the “Junk Punks” who wish to take over the planet.
  • Rewards involved items such as Food Dude stickers and pens.
  • Peer modelling and rewards were very effective.
  • Their consumption for fruits and vegetables went from 4% and 1% to 100% and 83%.
  • After 6 months, even though the procedure had stopped, they maintained the same level of consumption if not more.
